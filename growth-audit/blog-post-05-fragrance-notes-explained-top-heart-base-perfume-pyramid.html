If you only read one thing: A fragrance note list is a timeline, not an ingredient list. Top notes are what you smell in the first 5 to 30 minutes. Heart notes are what the fragrance becomes over the next one to three hours. Base notes are what lingers for the rest of the day. When you spray something on a blotter strip at a department-store counter, you are smelling only the top notes — which is why that first impression so often leads to expensive regret. Spray it on skin, wait 30 minutes, then decide.

TL;DR — the at-a-glance reference

Note tier When you smell it Typical duration Common materials Role in the composition
Top Immediately on application 5–30 minutes Citrus (bergamot, lemon, grapefruit), light herbs, pink pepper, aldehydes First impression; sets the opening mood
Heart / middle After top notes recede 30 minutes to 2–4 hours Florals (rose, jasmine, geranium), spices (cardamom, cinnamon), fruits (pineapple, peach, plum) The main character; the scent’s identity
Base As heart notes recede 2–8+ hours Woods (sandalwood, cedarwood, oud), musks, resins (benzoin, labdanum), vanilla, amber, patchouli The dry-down; what lingers on skin all day

The table is intentionally simplified. Reality is messier — notes do not arrive and depart on a train schedule, and not every fragrance follows the pyramid model faithfully. The sections below get into that complexity.

What a fragrance “note” actually is

The word “note” in fragrance is borrowed from music, and the analogy is instructive up to a point. Just as a musical note is a single, identifiable tone within a larger chord, a fragrance note is a single, identifiable scent impression within a larger composition. But there the analogy becomes imprecise, because what is listed as a single “note” on a product page can be one of three very different things.

A single raw material. Bergamot, vetiver, and rose absolute are real plant-derived ingredients. When they appear in a note list, there is a corresponding ingredient in the formula — extracted from the plant source by distillation, solvent extraction, or cold expression.

A synthetic aroma chemical. Ambroxan (the lab-synthesized analogue of ambergris), Iso E Super (the woody-cedarwood synthetic used at bold concentrations in Escentric Molecules), hedione (a diffusive jasmine-like molecule present in enormous amounts in Chânel No. 19 and many others) — these are not natural extracts. They are laboratory-engineered compounds, and they are responsible for some of the most iconic performances in modern luxury perfumery. They appear on note lists in exactly the same format as naturals, with no distinction made.

An accord. “Amber” is almost always an accord — a blend of resins, musks, and vanilla constructed to read as a single warm, resinous impression. “Wood” in a note list usually means a blend of woody aroma chemicals rather than a single extracted wood material. An accord is the perfumer’s building block, but it presents as a single term on a note pyramid.

This distinction matters for a specific practical reason: the note list on a product page reflects what the perfumer and brand want you to perceive, not a precise ingredient disclosure. Regulatory ingredient declarations list raw materials in a format nearly impossible to read as a consumer. The note pyramid is a communication tool, not a formula. It is a map of the intended olfactory experience over time. Understanding it as a timeline — rather than as a recipe — is the frame that makes it useful.

Top notes: the opening 5 to 30 minutes

Top notes are the most volatile aromatic compounds in a fragrance — meaning they evaporate from skin fastest, which is why you smell them first and why they disappear first. The chemistry is not complicated: smaller, lighter molecules have lower boiling points and escape into the air more readily. Citrus materials are the quintessential top notes for this reason. Bergamot, lemon, grapefruit, and mandarin contain limonene, linalool, and similar light terpene compounds that flash off quickly and brilliantly.

Common top note categories and what they contribute:

  • Citrus: Bergamot, lemon, grapefruit, yuzu, orange. Bright, clean, immediately inviting. The most common fragrance opening in Western luxury perfumery, because citrus reads as fresh and unintimidating to the nose — which is exactly what a designer wants when a stranger is forming a first impression at a department-store counter.
  • Light herbs and aromatics: Basil, mint, lavender in its lighter aspect. Clean and green, with an energizing quality that transitions well into floral or spice hearts.
  • Light spice: Pink pepper, cardamom at lower concentrations. These add energy and a faintly edgy quality to the opening without the heaviness of deeper spice notes that appear in the heart.
  • Aldehydes: The synthetic materials that gave 20th-century classics their powdery, soapy, almost effervescent opening character. Chânel No. 5 is the textbook example — that initial burst of bright, abstract, slightly metallic quality is aldehyde.

The practical implication is significant: if you evaluate a fragrance on a strip or on the back of your hand immediately after spraying, you are forming an opinion based almost entirely on top notes. That is a meaningful limitation. The fragrance you are about to spend $350 on — the thing that will sit on your skin for six hours — has barely appeared yet.

A concrete example from the Base Note catalog: Creed Aventus opens with bergamot, black currant, apple, lemon, and pink pepper. That opening is bright and tart-fruity. If you encountered Aventus on a blotter for 45 seconds and moved on, you would have a reasonable first impression of a citrus-fruity fragrance — and essentially no information about the pineapple-patchouli heart or the birch-and-ambroxan dry-down that define what Aventus actually is. The opening is real, but it is the least distinctive part of the experience.

Heart notes: the main character, 30 minutes to four hours

Heart notes — sometimes called middle notes — are the body of the fragrance. They emerge as the top notes dissipate, and they represent the longest-lasting and typically the most complex phase of the fragrance experience. Most perfumers treat the heart as the composition’s identity: the mood they are building, the story they are telling.

Common heart note categories:

  • Florals: Rose, jasmine, geranium, ylang-ylang, iris. Florals are the most common heart-note category across all of Western perfumery history. Even in fragrances marketed as unambiguously masculine and non-floral, a floral heart element often provides structural complexity and warmth without reading as “floral” to the wearer.
  • Warm spices: Cinnamon, clove, nutmeg. These heavier spices have lower volatility than the pink pepper and cardamom used in top notes, and they bloom naturally as the opening fades.
  • Fruity notes: Pineapple, peach, plum, apple in its richer aspect. When a fruit note persists past the first 15 minutes, it is operating as a heart element — a heavier molecular weight compound that the top-note version of the same fruit cannot match for longevity.
  • Herbs and green notes: Lavender in heavier concentration, sage, violet leaf. At low doses these function as top notes; at higher doses they anchor as heart elements with genuine staying power.

The heart is where skin chemistry begins to have real impact. The same fragrance on two people can read noticeably differently during the heart phase, because skin pH, temperature, and moisture influence how mid-volatility molecules interact with body chemistry. This is the primary reason “try before you commit to a full bottle” is not empty advice — the heart phase is personal in a way the opening often is not.

From the catalog: Parfums de Marly Layton opens with apple, bergamot, and lavender — a clean, slightly aromatic citrus introduction — and then unfolds into a heart of geranium, jasmine, and violet. That floral-herbaceous heart is what Layton actually smells like for most of the time you are wearing it. The opening is pleasant; the heart is distinctive. Smelling Layton for 30 seconds on a strip gives you a convincing picture of the first and almost no picture of the second.

Base notes: the dry-down and the lasting impression

Base notes are the lowest-volatility components of a fragrance — heavy, slow-evaporating molecules that often do not become fully perceptible until the top and heart notes have receded. The dry-down — the term the fragrance community uses for the late stages of a fragrance on skin — is composed almost entirely of base materials.

Common base note categories:

  • Woods: Sandalwood, cedarwood, guaiac wood, oud (agarwood). Heavy, persistent, and deeply varied. Sandalwood reads as creamy and warm; cedarwood as dry and slightly astringent; oud, in its more extreme expressions, as animalic, smoky, and medicinal. No two wood materials smell alike.
  • Musks: Natural musks from animal glands are now nearly universally replaced by synthetic alternatives for ethical and regulatory reasons. Synthetic musks — white musk, cashmeran, galaxolide, Habanolide — provide the skin-adjacent, slightly clean, slightly warm quality that makes a fragrance feel like “you” rather than something sitting on top of you. Many of the most-loved base-note signatures in contemporary niche perfumery are built from synthetic musks.
  • Resins and balsams: Benzoin, labdanum, myrrh, frankincense, opoponax. These ancient materials provide the deep warmth and richness that Oriental and Amber-family fragrances depend on. Labdanum in particular — a resin from the cistus plant — is one of the foundational materials in the chypre fragrance family.
  • Vanilla and tonka bean: These sweeter base elements appear in a remarkable percentage of modern luxury fragrances. Tonka bean, with its coumarin-driven warm, almond-like character, is one of the most common base notes in contemporary niche perfumery for men and unisex presentations.
  • Patchouli and vetiver: Two of the most important base materials in fine perfumery. Patchouli reads as earthy and slightly sweet; vetiver as smoky, dry, and rooty. Both anchor fragrances in a way that gives them character and a sense of weight that lighter materials cannot replicate.
  • Amber: Almost always an accord rather than a single material — typically a warm, resinous, slightly sweet blend used to smooth and unify the dry-down. When you see “amber” in a base, you are being told that the dry-down is warm and enveloping, not that a specific amber material is present.

Base notes are where longevity lives. If a fragrance has good sillage — the trail it leaves in the air as you move through a room — four or six hours after application, it is coming from base note materials. A fragrance with excellent top and heart notes but weak base notes will feel brief and insubstantial. A fragrance with heavy base notes can last all day and transfer to clothing long after you have forgotten you applied it.

What the pyramid does not tell you

The perfume pyramid is useful, but it has real limitations worth saying plainly rather than burying in caveats.

It assumes linearity. The model presents fragrance evolution as a clean sequence: tops fade, hearts emerge, bases persist. Many modern fragrances — particularly from niche and experimental houses — are built to smell like a coherent whole from application through dry-down, not a series of discrete stages. A well-constructed accord can feel relatively consistent over hours. Some perfumers actively reject the pyramid model as an outdated description of how contemporary perfumery works. When you see a note list with no top/heart/base division at all, that is often a deliberate statement by the house.

It omits concentration context. A note that appears in the heart tier of an Eau de Toilette formulation might appear in the top tier of the Eau de Parfum version of the same fragrance, because the concentration ratio changes how quickly different materials volatilize relative to each other. Two bottles labeled identically can have note pyramids that unfold differently if one is an EdT and one is an EdP.

It is a marketing document, not a formula. The house — not a neutral third party — decides which notes to list and at which tier. Some houses list a note in the heart that is present in tiny concentrations purely for effect. Others omit synthetic aroma chemicals (which constitute the majority of most modern fragrances’ formulas) entirely, presenting an impression of naturality that may not match the actual composition. The note list tells you what the house wants you to think the fragrance smells like. That is useful, but not the same as complete.

Skin chemistry changes everything in the heart and base. Particularly for musks and certain resins, what happens on your skin at hour four is highly personal. The pyramid tells you what to expect on average. It does not predict your specific experience.

How to actually use the pyramid when sampling

Given all of the above, here is the practical application. When thinking about how to build a fragrance rotation — which is the core reason we built Base Note the way we did — we suggest using the pyramid in this order.

Read the base notes first. They are what you will be wearing for the majority of the time a fragrance is on your skin. If the base list includes oud, incense, and civet and you find animalic fragrances overwhelming, you know something important before you ever open the bottle. If the base notes are sandalwood, vanilla, and white musk and you love warm, approachable skin scents, you have a reasonable prior on whether this fragrance is worth your time.

Read the heart notes second, because they are the fragrance’s identity. Look for the materials that will be present during the longest phase of wear — the one to four hours after the opening settles. A heavy floral heart you find cloying will follow you all day.

Read the top notes last, and give them the least weight. They matter for the first impression, but you are not wearing the first impression. You are wearing the full evolution.

Then, regardless of what the pyramid told you, spray on skin and wait 30 minutes before forming a real opinion. The pyramid is a prediction. Your skin is the actual test. This is precisely why wearing a fragrance via a sample atomizer before committing to a full bottle makes financial sense — as our broader comparison of fragrance subscription services covers in detail, the value of a subscription format is the ability to wear a fragrance across its full evolution, across different days and temperatures, before spending $300–500 on a bottle.

Let’s apply this concretely to three fragrances we carry, using their published note pyramids to illustrate what the model tells you — and where it falls short of the real experience.

Tom Ford Oud Wood

  • Top: Rosewood, cardamom, pepper
  • Heart: Oud, sandalwood, vetiver
  • Base: Tonka bean, amber, vanilla

Reading this bottom-up: a warm, sweet base (tonka, vanilla, amber) anchored to a rich woody-animalic heart (oud, sandalwood, vetiver), with a warm and spiced opening — no citrus, no bright entry. This tells you before you ever spray it that Tom Ford Oud Wood is a heavy, intimate, evening-weight fragrance with warmth in every phase. In this case the pyramid is unusually accurate: Oud Wood is almost linear in its evolution — it smells like a coherent whole from the first spray through hour eight. What the pyramid cannot tell you is that this is one of the most immediately recognizable luxury fragrances on the market, which is either an asset or a liability depending on how you feel about wearing something that fragrance-aware people will clock immediately.

Xerjoff Erba Pura

  • Top: Sicilian mandarin, bergamot, yuzu, lemon
  • Heart: White musk, violet, lily
  • Base: Musk, amber, sandalwood, cotton musk

Reading this bottom-up: an exceptionally clean, musky base with light wood and amber support. The heart is dominated by white musk and light florals. The top is pure citrus. This tells you Erba Pura is a clean, skin-close fragrance that prioritizes approachability and transparent brightness over complexity or projection. It will not announce itself across a room; it will smell excellent up close. The pyramid correctly predicts this character, but what it cannot communicate is the specific synthetic musk construction — the combination of molecules responsible for Erba Pura’s clean, almost solar quality — which is genuinely novel and worth experiencing to understand. The pyramid gives you the category. The actual wear gives you the specificity.

Parfums de Marly Layton

  • Top: Apple, bergamot, lavender
  • Heart: Geranium, jasmine, violet
  • Base: Vanilla, sandalwood, pepper, patchouli, guaiac wood

Reading this bottom-up: a richly warm, slightly smoky base (patchouli, guaiac wood) sweetened with vanilla and grounded by sandalwood, built on a floral-herbaceous heart (geranium, jasmine), with a clean citrus-aromatic opening. The pyramid correctly signals that Layton is a crowd-pleasing, slightly gourmand floral-fougère — warm, structured, broadly accessible. What it cannot tell you is how well those layers integrate. Layton is exceptional because the transitions between tiers are unusually smooth, and the dry-down is richer and more complex than the base list alone would suggest. You have to wear it to get there. This is the limitation of every note pyramid: it describes the ingredients of the journey, not the quality of the travel.

Frequently asked questions

Do all fragrances have the same three tiers?

No. The three-tier pyramid is a convention, not a law of physics. It is an industry-standard communication format adopted widely across Western fine perfumery, but some houses — particularly at the niche and avant-garde end of the market — reject it. When you see a note list with no top/heart/base division, that is often a deliberate signal that the fragrance is conceived as a coherent accord rather than a linear sequence. Some houses also publish two-tier pyramids (opening & dry-down) or five-tier pyramids that subdivide the heart. There is no single governing authority that standardizes the format. Fragrantica is the most comprehensive community resource for cross-referencing official pyramids with user interpretation.

If I hate a specific note, should I avoid any fragrance that lists it?

Not automatically. Notes function differently at different concentrations and in different accords. Patchouli is the most common example: it is present at low concentrations in an enormous number of fragrances that people who “hate patchouli” enjoy without knowing it. At low concentration blended into an accord, patchouli reads as earthy depth rather than the heavy, pungent quality associated with raw patchouli oil. Similarly, oud at low concentration in a blended accord reads as dark and exotic; at high concentration in an oudy Arabic-style composition, it can read as medicinal or animalic. Let the note list orient you, but do not let it function as a veto before you have tested the fragrance on skin.

Why do fragrances smell different on skin versus on a strip?

Because skin is alive. A blotter strip holds the fragrance suspended in the air with no chemistry of its own. Your skin has temperature, pH, moisture, and sebum — all of which interact with aromatic molecules differently. The interaction affects evaporation rate (warmer skin accelerates it), which changes the ratio of materials you smell at any given moment. It also affects how some molecules bind to skin proteins, which changes their character in the heart and base phases. Two people can wear the same fragrance and smell meaningfully different from each other by the second hour. This is why we always recommend reading about the note pyramid before sampling, then sampling on skin before committing, regardless of how good the pyramid sounds on paper.

Should I evaluate a fragrance differently at a counter versus at home?

Yes, significantly. Counter evaluations — spray, wait 30 seconds, form opinion — are top-note evaluations. They are useful for ruling out obvious misfits (if the opening immediately repels you, the base is unlikely to save it), but they are not evaluations of the full fragrance. A proper evaluation means wearing it on skin for at least 20 to 30 minutes to reach the heart, and ideally for a full day to reach and assess the dry-down. Doing that across multiple candidates in a single department-store visit is physically impossible. It is the core practical argument for sampling via a small atomizer before committing to a full bottle — which our piece on fragrance subscription alternatives covers from a cost-efficiency perspective.

The bottom line

The perfume pyramid is a genuinely useful tool for one specific purpose: understanding, before you spend money, what kind of fragrance experience you are likely to have in the hours after the opening. Read it bottom-up. Weight the base and heart more heavily than the top. Use it to build a working hypothesis before you spray the bottle. Then test that hypothesis on your own skin — because skin chemistry, concentration, and the specific way individual accords are constructed will produce a result that the pyramid predicts but cannot fully describe.

The fragrance industry has built sophisticated systems for communicating what a perfume does across its full evolution. The note pyramid is the one that survived into mass consumer communication because it is legible to someone who has never studied perfumery. It is worth learning to read correctly. The alternative — forming judgments based on 45-second strip impressions in a store — is how people end up with expensive full bottles that smell great for the first 10 minutes and then become something they did not want on their skin for the rest of the day.

Read the pyramid. Then test the pyramid on your skin. That order matters more than it sounds.
